# Conversion Principles Library

The core principles behind every effective CRO decision — with examples and application notes.

---

## The Five Conversion Levers

Every conversion problem traces back to one of these five levers. Diagnose which one is broken before prescribing a fix.

| Lever | Question It Answers | Common Symptom |
|---|---|---|
| **Clarity** | Do they understand what this is? | High bounce, low scroll depth |
| **Relevance** | Is this for them? | High traffic, low engagement |
| **Friction** | Is it too hard to convert? | Drop-off mid-funnel, form abandonment |
| **Anxiety** | Do they trust this? | Visitors reach CTA but don't click |
| **Motivation** | Do they want this enough, right now? | Returning visits, no conversion |

---

## Clarity Principles

### The 5-Second Rule
A visitor should be able to answer these three questions within 5 seconds of landing:
1. What is this?
2. Who is it for?
3. What do I do next?

If they can't — fix the headline and CTA before anything else.

### Above the Fold Priority
Everything above the fold should drive a single action. No competing messages, no navigation to unrelated pages, no distraction.

### Specificity Wins
Specific claims convert better than vague ones. "Reduce reporting time by 73%" outperforms "Save time on reporting."

---

## Relevance Principles

### Message Match
The headline on a landing page must match the language of the ad, email, or search result that sent the visitor there. Any mismatch = confusion = bounce.

**Bad:** Ad says "Free project management tool for agencies" → Page headline says "Work smarter with your team"
**Good:** Ad says "Free project management tool for agencies" → Page headline says "The free project tool agencies actually use"

### Traffic-Specific Pages
High-volume paid campaigns deserve their own landing pages. A homepage trying to serve every visitor type converts poorly for all of them.

### ICP Specificity
Broad audiences get broad copy. Specific audiences get specific copy. "For marketing teams at B2B SaaS companies" converts better than "For teams."

## Friction Principles

### Minimum Viable Form
Every field you add to a form costs conversions. Remove every field that isn't essential to follow up. Best practice: name + email only for lead gen, email only for newsletter.

### Progressive Disclosure
Don't ask for everything upfront. Collect the minimum to get started, then ask for more over time.

### Cognitive Load
The more choices a visitor has to make, the more likely they are to make none. Simplify navigation, reduce plan options, highlight a recommended choice.

### Page Speed
Every 1 second of additional load time reduces conversions by ~7%. Mobile users abandon pages loading > 3 seconds.

### Mobile Friction Points
- Tap targets too small (< 44px)
- Forms hard to fill on mobile keyboard
- CTAs below the fold on mobile
- Pop-ups that cover content and are hard to dismiss

## Anxiety Principles

### Social Proof Hierarchy
Listed in decreasing specificity (more specific = more credible):

1. Named customer quotes with outcome metrics
2. Named customer quotes without metrics
3. Named customers without quotes (logo bar)
4. Anonymous testimonials with title/company
5. Review platform ratings (G2, Capterra) with count
6. User/customer count ("10,000+ teams")
7. Media mentions

### Trust Signals Checklist
- [ ] HTTPS / security badge for payment forms
- [ ] Money-back guarantee or free trial
- [ ] "No credit card required" near CTAs
- [ ] Privacy statement near email fields
- [ ] Real company address and contact info
- [ ] Founded date / team photos (signals legitimacy)
- [ ] Press mentions

### Risk Reversal
The CTA should reduce perceived risk:
- "Start free — no credit card required"
- "Try free for 14 days. Cancel anytime."
- "30-day money-back guarantee. No questions asked."

## Motivation Principles

### Urgency (Real Only)
Manufactured countdown timers and fake "only 3 left" messages damage trust when visitors see through them. Only use urgency if it's real:
- Limited-time pricing (with end date)
- Cohort-based courses with real enrollment deadlines
- Event registrations with capacity limits

### Scarcity
Real scarcity converts. Fake scarcity backfires. If spots are genuinely limited — say so with specifics.

### Primary + Secondary CTA
Not every visitor is ready to buy. Give high-intent visitors a clear primary CTA ("Start Free Trial") and not-ready visitors a lower-commitment option ("See how it works →").

### Loss Framing
Loss aversion is powerful. What does the visitor *lose* by not converting?
- "Teams without [Product] spend 6 hours a week on [pain]. That's 300 hours a year."

## Page-Level Benchmarks

Use these as orientation points, not targets. Every product, audience, and traffic source varies.

| Page Type | Typical CVR Range |
|---|---|
| Homepage (to signup) | 2–5% |
| Landing page (paid traffic) | 5–15% |
| Pricing page (to trial) | 8–20% |
| Lead capture / content offer | 20–40% |
| Free tool (to email capture) | 15–35% |

If you're significantly below these ranges, you have a Clarity or Friction problem.
If you're within range but want to grow, focus on Motivation and Anxiety.

## A/B Test Prioritisation

Before writing a test, answer:
1. What is the hypothesis? ("We believe [change] will [outcome] because [reason]")
2. What is the baseline conversion rate?
3. How much traffic does this page get? (< 500 conversions/month = underpowered)
4. What is the minimum detectable effect you care about?
5. How long do you need to run the test to reach significance?

**Priority order for tests:**
1. Headline (biggest impact, fastest signal)
2. CTA copy and placement
3. Hero image / visual
4. Social proof type and placement
5. Form length
6. Pricing structure / plan presentation
7. Page length and content order

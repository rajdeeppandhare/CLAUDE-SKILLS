# 🎯 Decision OS

### A Full Cognitive Operating System for High-Stakes Decisions

---

## What Is This?

**Decision OS** is a Claude skill that transforms how you think through important
choices. Instead of listing pros and cons (which just reorganises your existing
biases), it runs your decision through **9 analytical frameworks** — each designed
to surface a different dimension of the choice that simple thinking misses.

The result isn't just a recommendation. It's a structured breakdown that shows you
**why** one path is better, **what you'll give up** by taking it, **where it could
fail**, and **what cognitive distortions** may be shaping how you're framing the
choice in the first place.

---

## Why Pros & Cons Lists Don't Work

A pros and cons list has two fundamental problems:

**1. It treats all factors as equal.**
"New city" as a con on one side of the page has the same visual weight as
"leaves family and 10-year friendships behind" — even though those things are
not remotely equivalent.

**2. It just reflects what's already in your head.**
You write the list. You weight it. You unconsciously curate it toward the
answer you've already emotionally decided on. The list confirms rather than challenges.

Decision OS is built to do the opposite: to surface what you're not thinking about,
to force weight to matter, and to check your reasoning against frameworks that
don't care what you've already decided.

## The 9-Framework Pipeline

```
      ▼

      │
      ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐

│         │ What is permanently given up by each choice?          │

      │
      ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Stage 3 │ Risk Scoring                                          │
│         │ Upside / Downside / Catastrophe on each option.       │

      │
      ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Stage 4 │ Time & Energy Investment Analysis                     │
│         │ What does each option cost in life energy?            │

└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
      │
      ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Stage 5 │ Regret Minimisation Framework                         │
│         │ Project to age 80 and look back.                      │

└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
      │
      ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ Stage 6 │ Identity & Values Alignment Check                     │

      │
      ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐

│         │ Cognitive biases flagged.                             │

```

## What You Get in the Verdict

The synthesis (Stage 9) produces:

- **Framework scoreboard** — which option each framework favours
- **Verdict + confidence level** — clear, direct recommendation
- **The insight you might have missed** — the thing the analysis surfaced that you probably didn't frame this way
- **Key condition** — the one variable that, if it changes, changes the answer
- **First action** — the single highest-leverage thing to do if you go with the winner
- **Early warning sign** — what to watch for in the first 3–6 months
- **Cognitive biases flagged** — how your framing may be distorting your thinking

## What Makes This Different from Asking for Pros & Cons

| Pros & Cons | Decision OS |
|-------------|------------|
| All factors treated equally | Factors weighted by what actually matters to you |
| Reflects your existing thinking | Challenges and extends your thinking |
| No time dimension | Explicit time and energy cost analysis |
| No failure analysis | Pre-mortem surfaces failure modes before you commit |
| No cognitive bias check | Biases detected and named |
| Looks one step ahead | Maps 3 moves ahead (2nd order consequences) |
| No regret framing | Explicit age-80 regret projection |
| No identity check | Flags identity debt before you take it on |
| Conclusion: vague | Verdict: direct, with confidence level |

## The Frameworks — Source Concepts

Each stage is grounded in established thinking:

| Stage | Framework | Source Concept |
|-------|-----------|---------------|
| 1 | Weighted Decision Matrix | Operations research / multi-criteria decision analysis |
| 2 | Opportunity Cost | Classical economics (Ricardo, Marshall) |
| 3 | Risk Asymmetry | Nassim Taleb — asymmetric payoffs |
| 4 | Life Energy Analysis | "Your Money or Your Life" — Dominguez & Robin |
| 5 | Regret Minimisation | Jeff Bezos / 10-10-10 Suzy Welch |
| 6 | Identity Alignment | Identity-based habits (James Clear) + Erikson's stages |
| 7 | Second-Order Thinking | Howard Marks / Charlie Munger mental models |
| 8 | Pre-Mortem | Gary Klein / Daniel Kahneman |
| 9 | Cognitive Bias Detection | Kahneman "Thinking, Fast and Slow" + Cialdini |

## When It Triggers

The skill activates on:

- Direct decision questions: "Should I X or Y?", "Help me decide between…"
- Fork-in-the-road situations: "I'm torn between…", "I can't choose…"
- Life event triggers: Career change, job offers, startup consideration, relocation, education decisions, major purchases
- Implicit trade-offs: Any description of a situation where two paths are present, even if the user doesn't explicitly ask for a decision framework
- Explicit requests: "Help me think through this", "What would you do?", "Is it worth it?"

---

## Adaptive Depth

Not every decision needs the full pipeline. The skill adapts:

| Stakes Level | What runs |
|-------------|-----------|
| Life-defining, irreversible | All 9 stages in full |
| High stakes, costly to reverse | All 9, stages 6–7 compressed |
| Medium stakes, reversible | Stages 1, 3, 5, 9 |
| Low stakes, easily reversible | Stage 1 + "just run the experiment" advice |

For low-stakes reversible decisions, the skill will tell you:
"This is actually a cheap experiment. Pick one, run it for 30 days, use the
data to decide for real." That's often more valuable than a full framework.

## Limitations

- **This skill analyses, it doesn't decide for you.** The verdict is a strong recommendation, not a command. You have context the analysis doesn't.
- **Quality scales with input.** The more specific you are about what matters to you, the better the matrix weights and the more accurate the identity alignment check.
- **Some decisions need more than a framework.** Relationship decisions in particular benefit from this analysis but ultimately involve emotional knowing that no framework fully captures.
- **The skill can't know what it doesn't know.** If you have critical private context (health situation, family dynamics, financial obligations), factor those into how you receive the verdict.

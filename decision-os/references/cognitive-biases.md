# Cognitive Biases — Detection Guide

Used in Stage 9 of the Decision OS pipeline to flag how the user may be
thinking about their decision in ways that distort the analysis.

Flag the 1–2 most relevant biases in the synthesis. Be direct but not preachy.
One sentence per bias: name it, and say specifically how it might be showing up.

---

## The Most Common Decision Biases

### 1. Status Quo Bias
**What it is:** Overvaluing the current situation simply because it's familiar.
**How it shows up:** "I know what I have now" / strong reluctance to change despite good reasons to
**Flag when:** The user defends their current path more than they evaluate it
**Counter:** "If you were starting from scratch today with no existing commitments, which would you choose?"

---

### 2. Loss Aversion
**What it is:** Losses feel roughly 2× as painful as equivalent gains feel good (Kahneman).
**How it shows up:** Overweighting what you'd give up vs what you'd gain; focusing on downside
**Flag when:** The user is more focused on what they'd lose than what they'd gain by each option
**Counter:** "If the downside risk were the same on both options, which upside would you choose?"

---

### 3. Sunk Cost Fallacy
**What it is:** Letting past investment influence future decisions it shouldn't affect.
**How it shows up:** "I've already put 3 years into this" / "I can't quit now after everything I've invested"
**Flag when:** User mentions prior time/money/effort as a reason to continue
**Counter:** "Ignore everything that's already happened. From today, with a clean slate — which path?"

---

### 4. Confirmation Bias
**What it is:** Seeking and remembering information that confirms the choice you've already made.
**How it shows up:** User has clearly already decided but frames it as an open question; evidence cited is suspiciously one-sided
**Flag when:** The framing of the decision contains loaded language favouring one option
**Counter:** "What's the strongest possible case for the option you're leaning away from?"

---

### 5. Present Bias (Hyperbolic Discounting)
**What it is:** Overweighting immediate costs and benefits vs future ones.
**How it shows up:** Avoiding short-term discomfort of a choice that's clearly better long-term; "I just need to get through this year first"
**Flag when:** The user keeps postponing the decision or choosing comfort over growth
**Counter:** "If the discomfort of Option A happened to someone else, and you were advising them — what would you say?"

---

### 6. Overconfidence Bias
**What it is:** Overestimating your ability to predict, execute, or succeed.
**How it shows up:** "I know this will work" / unrealistically optimistic timelines / underestimating execution difficulty
**Flag when:** Success probability estimates feel inflated; no failure mode consideration
**Counter:** Run the pre-mortem (Stage 8) — it's specifically designed to counter overconfidence

---

### 7. Underconfidence / Imposter Syndrome
**What it is:** Systematically underestimating your own capability, especially for bold options.
**How it shows up:** "I'm not sure I'm ready" / "Someone else could probably do this better" / deferring to others' judgment about your own capabilities
**Flag when:** User is clearly qualified but second-guessing themselves; hesitation is about self-belief not evidence
**Counter:** "If a friend with your exact experience described this situation to you, what would you tell them?"

---

### 8. Anchoring
**What it is:** Over-relying on the first piece of information encountered.
**How it shows up:** The first number, offer, or comparison in the conversation becomes the reference point for everything else
**Flag when:** User is comparing to an arbitrary reference point (first salary offered, cost of competitor, what a friend did)
**Counter:** "Remove that number from the equation. What would you think if you saw this fresh?"

---

### 9. Planning Fallacy
**What it is:** Underestimating how long and how much things will cost; optimism about execution.
**How it shows up:** "It'll probably take 6 months" (it will take 18); "I can handle that while doing my job" (usually can't)
**Flag when:** Timeline or cost estimates are round, confident, and don't account for setbacks
**Counter:** "What's the realistic case — not best case? What happened to similar projects you or others undertook?"

---

### 10. FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)
**What it is:** Choosing an option primarily because others are doing it or it seems exciting, not because it's right for you.
**How it shows up:** "Everyone in my peer group is doing MBAs" / "Startups are hot right now" / "I feel like I'm falling behind"
**Flag when:** Motivation seems to be peer comparison rather than intrinsic desire
**Counter:** "If none of your peers existed — if this were purely your decision in isolation — which would you choose?"

---

### 11. Narrative Bias
**What it is:** Preferring the option that makes for a better story, regardless of expected value.
**How it shows up:** Choosing the startup because "it's a better story" / MBA because "it sounds impressive" / the dramatic path over the sensible one
**Flag when:** Prestige, narrative, or how it sounds to others seems to be a primary driver
**Counter:** "Strip out the story. Which option produces the life you actually want to live, not the one you want to describe?"

---

### 12. Social Proof Bias
**What it is:** Deferring to what others in your reference group are doing as a shortcut for what's right.
**How it shows up:** "My mentor says I should..." / "Most people in my position do X" / "My parents think..."
**Flag when:** User is citing external opinion rather than their own values and evidence
**Counter:** "You are not most people. What do YOU think, separate from what others expect of you?"

---

## How to Raise Biases Without Being Preachy

Good: "One thing worth noting: the way you've described this suggests some loss aversion might be at play — you've mentioned what you'd give up three times, but only mentioned what you'd gain once. Worth checking if that framing is proportionate to the actual risks."

Bad: "You are exhibiting classic loss aversion bias which is causing you to make an irrational decision."

The goal is to illuminate, not lecture. Name the bias, describe specifically how it appears in their framing, and give them the reframe question. Then let them decide.

## 1. Weighted Decision Matrix — Advanced Tips

**Choosing criteria:**
- Always include at least one emotional/qualitative criterion (e.g. "excites me", "fits who I want to be")
- Never more than 8 criteria — beyond that, weights become meaningless
- Criteria should be independent (avoid double-counting the same thing)

**Weighting:**
- Ask the user: "If you could only optimise for one thing, what would it be?" → highest weight (0.25–0.35)
- The remaining weights should reflect genuine trade-offs
- If a user weights everything equally (0.125 each), push back — it usually means they haven't decided what matters

**Scoring calibration:**
- 9–10: Exceptional, rare
- 7–8: Good, above average
- 5–6: Acceptable, neither good nor bad
- 3–4: Below average, problematic
- 1–2: Poor, major concern

**Interpreting the result:**
- Margin > 1.5: Strong signal in favour of winner
- Margin 0.5–1.5: Moderate signal, other frameworks matter more
- Margin < 0.5: Matrix is effectively tied; go deeper on regret and identity

**When to distrust the matrix:**
- When the user is clearly motivated to make one choice and has unconsciously weighted criteria to justify it → call this out
- When one option scores 9–10 on the most weighted criterion → that usually just wins

## 2. Opportunity Cost — The Hidden Calculation

**The core insight:**
Most people calculate the cost of choosing Option A as the price of A.
The actual cost is **the best thing they give up by not choosing B**.

**How to make it vivid:**
Don't just list abstract trade-offs. Describe the foregone life concretely:

> "If you choose the MBA, you're not just spending $150k — you're giving up
> 24 months of compounding startup experience, 24 months of product shipping,
> and potentially the founding moment of a company that could define your career."

**Time opportunity cost:**
Always calculate in time, not just money.
- How many months of life does this option consume at peak commitment?
- What else could those months have produced?

**The optionality value:**
Some options preserve optionality (keep more doors open); others close them.
An option that keeps more paths alive has hidden value beyond its direct payoff.
Quantify this qualitatively: "Option B keeps the MBA door open for 3 more years;
Option A closes it permanently after age 28 for most programmes."

## 3. Risk Scoring — Asymmetry Detection

**The key mental model:**
You want options with **positive asymmetry**:
- Upside: large, potentially unbounded
- Downside: painful but survivable and recoverable

Avoid options with **negative asymmetry**:
- Upside: modest and capped
- Downside: catastrophic or irreversible

**Categorising downsides:**
- **Recoverable**: Costs time/money/effort but you can come back from it
- **Costly to recover**: Sets you back significantly but not permanently
- **Irreversible**: Health damage, burned relationships, reputational destruction, financial ruin

**The catastrophe tier:**
Always ask: "What's the absolute worst realistic outcome, and what's the probability?"
If worst case is recoverable AND probability < 30%, the risk is usually worth taking.
If worst case is irreversible, no upside probability justifies it.

**Expected value framing:**
For each option:
EV = (Upside × P(success)) + (Downside × P(failure))

But note: EV is insufficient alone. A 10% chance of losing everything is not
acceptable just because EV is positive. Factor in ruin probability separately.

## 4. Time & Energy — The Life Energy Lens

**Source concept:**
From "Your Money or Your Life" (Dominguez/Robin): every expenditure of time is
an expenditure of life energy. Decisions that consume enormous time at low
meaning-per-hour are expensive in a way money can't capture.

**The audit questions:**
- How many hours per week does Option A demand at peak?
- For how many consecutive months?
- What life domains get crowded out? (sleep, relationships, health, creativity, leisure)
- What is the "recovery period" after this decision plays out?

**Hidden time costs to flag:**
- MBA: not just 2 years — factor recruiting season (Year 1 fall), thesis/capstone, networking events
- Startup: the "crunch before launch", fundraising rounds (each consuming 3–6 months)
- Career switch: skill gap period (6–18 months of lower productivity)
- Relocation: the first 6 months of social rebuild in a new city

**The energy ROI question:**
"For every hour I invest in this option, what am I getting back?"
Options that generate **energy** (meaning, momentum, mastery) as a byproduct of
the work are dramatically better than options that only drain it.

## 5. Regret Minimisation — Extended Bezos Framework

**Original framework:**
Jeff Bezos imagined himself at 80 looking back. He asked: "Will I regret not trying?"
For Amazon, the answer was yes. So he left DE Shaw.

**The extension — four scenarios:**
1. You chose A and it worked → [describe]
2. You chose A and it failed → [describe] — will you forgive yourself for trying?
3. You chose B and it worked → [describe]
4. You chose B and it failed → [describe]
5. You never decided / stayed in the default → [describe 5 years out] ← often the worst

**Key calibration:**
The question isn't "which option am I most likely to succeed at?"
It's "which failure would I be more at peace with?"

Most people fear the regret of trying and failing. In practice, people regret
**inaction far more than failed action** — especially for high-agency decisions.
Surface this directly: "Research consistently shows people regret the things
they didn't do more than the things they tried and failed at."

**The 10/10/10 variant (Suzy Welch):**
How will you feel about this decision:
- In 10 minutes?
- In 10 months?
- In 10 years?

If short-term fear (10 minutes) is driving the decision but long-term regret
(10 years) points the other way, flag this dissonance clearly.

## 6. Identity & Values Alignment

**Why this matters:**
A decision that's logically optimal but identity-misaligned will be:
- Hard to commit to fully
- Psychologically costly to execute
- Prone to self-sabotage

**Identity debt:**
When you make identity-misaligned choices repeatedly, you accumulate identity debt:
a growing gap between who you are and who the life you're building requires you to be.
Identity debt compounds. It shows up as burnout, resentment, and quiet quitting.

**The five identity dimensions:**
1. **Professional self** — What kind of worker/creator/leader do I see myself as?
2. **Relational self** — What kind of partner/parent/friend does this make me?
3. **Financial self** — Am I fundamentally a risk-taker or security-seeker?
4. **Freedom self** — Do I need autonomy or do I thrive in structure?
5. **Legacy self** — What do I want to have built or stood for?

**Practical question:**
"Does this option feel like the truest expression of who you're becoming,
or does it feel like a costume you're putting on?"

## 7. Second-Order Consequences

**The mental model:**
First-order thinking: "If I do X, Y happens."
Second-order thinking: "If Y happens, then Z happens."
Third-order thinking: "If Z happens, what world do I live in?"

**Why it matters for decisions:**
Most decisions are made on first-order outcomes.
Most regret comes from second and third-order consequences no one mapped.

**Common second-order blindspots:**
- **MBA path**: First-order = credential + network. Second-order = $150k debt changes risk tolerance. Third-order = you optimise for salary to service debt, constraining career choices for 5+ years.
- **Startup path**: First-order = exciting product building. Second-order = fundraising takes over as full-time job by Series A. Third-order = you become a CEO, not a builder — is that what you wanted?
- **Relocation**: First-order = new city, lower cost. Second-order = existing friendships atrophy from distance. Third-order = social network rebuild takes 2–3 years; this affects mental health and support systems.

**Doors that open:**
Map not just what each choice costs, but what new doors each one opens that
wouldn't exist otherwise. Sometimes a "worse" first-order choice unlocks better
second-order options.

## 8. Pre-Mortem Analysis

**Source:** Gary Klein / Daniel Kahneman

**The technique:**
Instead of asking "what could go wrong?" (which produces defensive, low-energy thinking),
say "it's 2 years from now and this decision was a disaster. What happened?"

This reframe unlocks more honest, specific failure mode identification.

**Standard failure categories to probe:**
- **Execution failure**: We had the right idea but couldn't deliver
- **Market/context failure**: External conditions changed against us
- **Resource failure**: Ran out of time, money, or energy
- **Motivation failure**: We lost belief in it / it stopped mattering
- **People failure**: Key relationships or collaborators failed
- **Information failure**: We made this decision on wrong assumptions

**The mitigation move:**
For each major failure mode identified, ask:
"What is the single action we could take NOW to reduce the probability of this?"

Pre-mortems are most valuable not for making you hesitate, but for making you
more resilient: you've already thought through the bad outcome, so it doesn't
blindside you.

## 9. Synthesis — Weighting Conflicting Frameworks

When frameworks point in different directions, use this hierarchy:

**Tiebreaker priority order:**
1. **Catastrophe check** (Stage 3): If one option has an irreversible catastrophic downside, it's eliminated regardless of other scores.
2. **Identity alignment** (Stage 6): A logically optimal choice that deeply misaligns with identity will fail in execution. Weight this heavily for personal decisions.
3. **Regret** (Stage 5): For irreversible decisions, regret minimisation is the strongest tie-breaker.
4. **Matrix + Risk** (Stages 1+3): For reversible, lower-stakes decisions, the quantitative frameworks carry more weight.
5. **Second-order** (Stage 7): When short-term outcomes look similar, long-term path divergence usually breaks the tie.

**Confidence calibration:**
- High confidence: 5+ frameworks point the same way
- Medium confidence: 3–4 frameworks agree, 1–2 dissent on non-critical dimensions
- Low confidence: Frameworks are split; more user information needed
- Needs more info: A critical variable is unknown that would swing the verdict

**The "one key condition" rule:**
Every verdict should name the single variable that, if it changed, would change
the recommendation. This makes the advice useful even as circumstances evolve.

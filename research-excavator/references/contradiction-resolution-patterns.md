# Contradiction Resolution Patterns

Used in Stage 5 of the Research Excavator pipeline. A taxonomy of why sources disagree, and how to present each type so the disagreement is clear without sounding like noise.

## Type 1: Stale vs. current data

**What it looks like**: Source A (older) gives one number; Source B (newer) gives a different one. This isn't really a contradiction — it's a timeline.

**How to present it**: "As of [date], X reported Y. This was updated/superseded by [newer source], which reports Z as of [later date]." Lead with the current figure, but keep the older one visible if the trend itself is informative (e.g., showing a rate of change).

## Type 2: Methodology differences

**What it looks like**: Two sources report different numbers for what sounds like the same thing, but they're actually measuring it differently (e.g., one survey counts contractors as employees, another doesn't; one study uses self-reported data, another uses verified records).

**How to present it**: Name the methodology difference explicitly rather than just presenting two numbers side by side. "Source A's figure of X uses [methodology]; Source B's figure of Y uses [different methodology] — they're not directly comparable." This is usually the single most valuable thing Stage 5 catches, because surface-level reading would just see "these disagree" without understanding why.

## Type 3: Regional or contextual variation

**What it looks like**: Sources disagree because they're describing different places, market segments, time periods, or populations, even though the question as asked sounds universal.

**How to present it**: Make the scope explicit for each claim. "In [region/segment], X. In [different region/segment], Y." If the user's question didn't specify which context they care about, flag that the answer depends on it.

## Type 4: Framing or institutional lean

**What it looks like**: Sources agree on the underlying facts but draw different conclusions or emphasize different implications because of an institutional or ideological stance (common in policy, health controversy-adjacent topics, and market commentary).

**How to present it**: Separate the factual claim (where there may be real agreement) from the interpretation (where the disagreement actually lives). "Both sources agree that [fact]. [Source A] frames this as [interpretation], while [Source B] frames it as [different interpretation] — note [Source A]'s known position on [topic] / [Source B]'s known position." Don't adjudicate which framing is "right" unless the evidence itself clearly supports one side; describe the disagreement accurately instead.

## Type 5: Single unverified claim vs. everything else

**What it looks like**: One source makes a claim that nothing else corroborates, and it conflicts with the broader consensus.

**How to present it**: Don't treat this as "50/50 he-said-she-said." State the consensus position clearly, then note the outlier claim and its lack of corroboration explicitly — "One source claims X, but this isn't corroborated elsewhere and conflicts with [consensus]." This is different from Type 4: here there usually IS a more credible position per the Stage 4 rubric, and the report should say so rather than presenting false balance.

## General principle

The goal of this stage is not to make every report sound contested — most well-established facts genuinely aren't, and manufacturing doubt where none exists is its own failure mode. The goal is to make sure that when disagreement *does* exist, it survives into the final report instead of getting silently resolved by whichever source got read first.

# Source-Type Weighting Guide

Used alongside Stage 4 in the Research Excavator pipeline. Which source types to actively seek out and weight more heavily, organized by research domain. This complements `credibility-scoring-rubric.md` — that file covers how to score any individual source, this one covers where to go looking in the first place.

## Technical / developer research

Seek out, roughly in priority order:
1. Official documentation and changelogs for the specific version in question
2. Maintainer commentary in GitHub issues, PRs, and discussions (often more honest about limitations than docs)
3. RFC/design docs if the project has them — these reveal *why* a decision was made, which matters for comparative questions
4. Accepted, well-voted Stack Overflow answers (check the date against current versions)
5. Conference talks or blog posts from people who've shipped the thing in production
6. Generic tutorial content (use for syntax confirmation, not for architectural claims)

Avoid leaning on SEO-optimized "Top 10 X vs Y" listicles as a primary source — they're frequently generated to rank, not to reflect hands-on experience, and tend to repeat the same generic talking points across dozens of near-identical articles.

## Market / financial research

Seek out, roughly in priority order:
1. Primary filings (10-K/10-Q, prospectuses, central bank statements, official economic releases)
2. Earnings call transcripts (management's own words, including the hedging)
3. Sell-side analyst notes — useful for synthesis, but always note the analyst's firm and whether they have a position
4. Reputable financial news for context and timeline
5. Aggregator/summary sites last, and only to find where the primary source is

Be alert to survivorship bias in "top performers" framing and to backtested strategies presented without out-of-sample validation.

## Health / medical research

Seek out, roughly in priority order:
1. Peer-reviewed studies (note sample size, whether it's a randomized trial vs. observational, and publication date)
2. Clinical guidelines from recognized national/international bodies
3. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses (generally stronger evidence than any single study)
4. Health journalism that names and quotes specific researchers/institutions
5. General wellness media and forums last, and clearly labeled as anecdotal if used at all

Always distinguish preliminary/early-stage research from established consensus, and note when a finding hasn't been replicated.

For anything that could read as personal medical guidance rather than general research, keep the output informational and suggest the person discuss specifics with a clinician — this skill produces research summaries, not medical advice.

## Policy / legal research

Seek out, roughly in priority order:
1. The actual text of the legislation, regulation, or court ruling
2. Government agency guidance and official interpretive documents
3. Analysis from named legal practitioners or academics (note their stated affiliations)
4. Advocacy and think-tank framing from multiple points on the spectrum — deliberately seek sources with different institutional leanings rather than the first few that come up
5. General news commentary last, mainly for timeline and public reaction context

Policy topics are the most prone to one-sided framing in search results. Treat the absence of a clearly opposing viewpoint in initial results as a signal to search more specifically for one, not as evidence that none exists.

## General / consumer research (products, travel, lifestyle)

Seek out, roughly in priority order:
1. Manufacturer/official specs for factual claims (dimensions, materials, included features)
2. Long-form reviews from outlets with a track record (check if they disclose how units were obtained)
3. Aggregated user review patterns (look at the distribution and recurring specific complaints, not just the star average)
4. Forums/communities for real-world failure modes and edge cases manufacturers don't mention
5. Be skeptical of any single "best X of 2026" listicle — these are often affiliate-driven and rotate products based on commission rates rather than tested quality

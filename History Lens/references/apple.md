# Apple — Archetype Reference

**Core muscle:** Ruthless prioritization — saying no as a strategic capability  
**Structural enablers:** Jobs's board authority and design-dictator status post-2000 return, sufficient margin (then later enormous margin) to be wrong on SKU count, vertical integration giving full control of quality at every layer, and a brand premium that let Apple charge more for fewer things

---

## The Archetype in One Sentence

Apple's core strategic capability is killing things — products, features, ports, categories — without apology, on the thesis that focus compounds better than breadth, and that a product defined by what it doesn't do is more coherent than one defined by everything it accommodates.

**This only works if:** You have the authority to make unpopular cuts stick (Jobs had it; most product managers don't), and the brand/margin to absorb short-term loss of the segments you've cut. A company at 5% market share cannot afford to be as selective as Apple at 40% margin.

---

## Case Study 1: The 1997 Product Line Purge

**Decision (1997):** Upon return, Jobs reduced Apple's product line from ~350 SKUs to 10. Killed the Newton (already shipping, with a dedicated user base), the LaserWriter, the Pippin, and a dozen peripheral lines. Drew a 2×2 on a whiteboard: Consumer/Pro × Desktop/Laptop. Four products. That's it.

**Why it was risky:** Apple was burning through cash. Cutting products meant cutting revenue lines. Employees, analysts, and the press thought it was suicidal. The Newton especially had passionate advocates who saw the kill as a personal betrayal.

**Structural enabler:** Jobs had board authority and had just negotiated a $150M investment from Microsoft that bought time. He could absorb the short-term analyst backlash in a way a less authoritative CEO couldn't. He also didn't care about the Newton user base because he was optimizing for what Apple could become, not what it owed its current users.

**Transferable principle:** "Product lines dilute organizational attention. Every product you keep is a vote you're making with engineering, marketing, and support hours. Cut until it hurts, then cut one more thing." The principle isn't minimalism for its own sake — it's that your team's focus is finite and every kept product is taxing it.

## Case Study 2: Dropping the Floppy Drive (iMac, 1998)

**Decision (1998):** The original iMac shipped with no floppy disk drive. This was genuinely controversial — the floppy was still the standard consumer file-transfer medium. Reviewers called it arrogant.

**Why it was risky:** Customers who relied on floppies had no upgrade path. Schools that used floppy-based curricula couldn't buy the iMac without a workaround. It generated real hostile press coverage.

**Structural enabler:** The iMac's target wasn't current Apple customers — it was Windows converts who were less attached to the floppy ecosystem. Jobs was designing for a future state (internet-first file sharing) not a current state (local file transfer).

**Transferable principle:** "Design for who you want to sell to next, not for who you've already sold to. The features you add to keep current users are often the features that prevent you from reaching the next wave." This is the Jobs version of Clayton Christensen's disruption thesis, applied at the product level.

## Case Study 3: Killing the 30-Pin Connector (Lightning, 2012)

**Decision (2012):** The iPhone 5 switched from the 30-pin dock connector (which had existed since 2003 and had a massive accessory ecosystem) to Lightning. Immediately obsoleted billions of dollars of accessories.

**Why it was risky:** The 30-pin ecosystem was the most valuable accessory ecosystem in consumer electronics history. Partners were furious. Customers who had invested in expensive docks and speakers faced immediate obsolescence.

**Structural enabler:** Apple's brand premium was strong enough that customers stayed anyway. And the accessory makers stayed too, because being excluded from the iPhone ecosystem was worse than having to re-tool. Apple had enough platform power that it could impose the transition cost on the ecosystem rather than absorb it.

**Transferable principle:** "When you have platform power, use it to make necessary transitions that your partners won't initiate voluntarily. But gauge your platform power honestly — this move only works if your users need you more than you need any single accessory partner."

## Graveyard: Apple Newton (1993–1998)

**What happened:** The Newton MessagePad was Apple's pre-Jobs handheld computer, positioned as a "personal digital assistant." It had genuinely innovative technology (early handwriting recognition, address book, calendar). It also had terrible handwriting recognition at launch, cost $700, and was famously mocked by Doonesbury. It never achieved mass adoption and was killed by Jobs in 1998.

**Why it failed:** The Newton was ahead of the market by ~10 years (Jobs killed it; the iPhone basically became it) but shipped too early, at too high a price, with the most-marketed feature (handwriting recognition) being visibly broken at launch. It was sold as a mass-market product before the technology could deliver the mass-market experience. Apple didn't kill things enough *internally* during development — it shipped a product defined by its aspiration, not its actual quality.

**Diagnostic question for your decision:** "Are we shipping this because it's genuinely ready, or because we've invested so much we're afraid to keep waiting?" The Newton is Apple's own lesson that even great long-term theses get destroyed by premature execution. The lesson isn't "don't ship bold ideas" — it's "don't let sunk cost override quality judgment."

**Contrast with the iMac:** The iMac also dropped features users wanted (floppy) but the *core experience* was genuinely excellent. The Newton dropped features users would have liked but the *core experience* was broken. Boldness in cutting features is different from shipping a broken core experience.

## When to Apply the Apple Lens

Apply it when the decision involves:
- What to cut, deprioritize, or kill
- Whether to serve a niche (current users) or a broader future market
- How to handle requests from current users that would compromise coherence for future users
- Whether to add another feature/SKU or hold the line

**Do not apply it when:**
- The user doesn't have the authority to make the cut stick culturally
- The user is early-stage with no brand premium — you need platform power for Apple-style leverage
- The decision is about what to build, not what to stop building
- The user's margin is thin enough that cutting revenue lines is existentially risky right now
